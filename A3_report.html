Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • The selection of a line chart format can better represent changes, here manifested as the differences in population distribution across different age groups;
  • Using gender as the grouping condition, the relationship between male and female lines can be used to determine the comparison of gender numbers (for instance, whether the number of males always exceeds that of females; or if the comparison of male to female numbers fluctuates over time—and differentiated by color) and to some extent visualize the gap (distance between points);
  • Two sets of lines (a total of 4 lines) can also facilitate the comparison of numbers between 2019 and 2020.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • The selection of a histogram format can reflect the population number through the area;
  • The selection of a population pyramid format can better represent the distribution of population numbers at both different gender and different age stages;
  • Four quadrants can add dimensions to the analysis—this chart includes a comparison of male & female, a comparison of population quantities across different age groups, and a comparison of data from 1900 and 2000.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • Attempted to simplify the information, only aiming to express the comparison of gender distribution;
  • Redefinition of the coordinate axes, with the number of females and males used as the x and y axes respectively, allows for a clearer representation of gender distribution through the spread of data points (and distinguishes gender by color contrast);
  • A 45-degree line of equality is used to assess the overall situation of gender quantity comparison (if a point lies above this line, it indicates that the number of females is greater than the number of males).

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

  • Based on the third sketch, I refined it using Tableau. I continued to use male population and female population as the x/y axes to better filter out other variables and focus solely on the age comparison. Meanwhile, I calculated a new variable, ratio (= female population/male population), and used the range of the ratio as a condition for color selection. A ratio greater than 1.05 indicates that the number of females significantly exceeds that of males (an unbalanced state), and is represented by a deep blue color; conversely, a deep red color is used for ratios less than 0.95. Ratios between 0.95 and 1.05 are considered balanced. Additionally, the size of the circle reflects the magnitude of the ratio, making it easier to visually compare the number of males and females across different age groups. The comparison of red and blue colors can also infer the overall gender balance distribution within the group. Furthermore, the years 2000 and 1900 were differentiated, with two charts displayed to better showcase the details visually.
  • Therefore, based on these two charts, we can draw some conclusions about the U.S. population gender balance: In 1900, the gender balance within the 0-20 and 55-80 age ranges was essentially balanced, while between the ages of 25-55, the number of females was significantly less than that of males. However, the gender distribution in 2000 was different, with a more balanced number of males and females before the age of 50, but becoming more imbalanced after 50: the number of males significantly exceeded that of females, surpassing the imbalance observed in the data from 1900.

Reflection

    Throughout the process of data visualization, I often find myself lost in an abundance of data, hoping to convey content across more dimensions, yet this can lead to the main theme I wish to express being captured less clearly. I believe the introduction of Tableau technology enables the generation of intermediary variables (such as ratio), thereby facilitating further filtering and presentation of information, which is difficult to achieve with hand-drawn methods. However, at the same time, the limitations of my own technical skills actually restrict my choice of graphical forms. For instance, in this case, I always wanted to use the Cartesian coordinate system to interpret data, due to my lack of core techniques for producing other types of images. This, to some extent, affects my thinking in visual design.
